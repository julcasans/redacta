## Allegories Hidden in Still Life

Before we dive into the heart of our series, let's pause to examine a fascinating example: Pieter Claesz's 1623 painting, a masterpiece from the Dutch Golden Age. At first glance, it appears to be a classic still life, but beneath the surface lies a rich allegory of the five senses. Many works from this era subtly reference the senses, even if today we simply categorize them as still lifes.

Claesz ingeniously weaves each sense into the composition:

- **Hearing**: Musical instruments evoke the sense of sound.
- **Sight**: The painting itself, along with a mirror reflecting a wine glass, draws attention to vision.
- **Touch**: A turtle, with its hard, textured shell, symbolizes tactile sensation.
- **Taste**: Sliced bread, cake, sweets, and wine represent flavors.
- **Smell**: The foods themselves, inviting us to imagine their aromas.

All five senses are present, seamlessly integrated into the still life format.

## Allegorical Figures: Personifying Concepts

Moving from objects to figures, consider the allegorical representations found throughout art history. The painting previously discussed in class focused on hearing, but in a very different manner: through personification. Here, allegories are embodied by female figuresâ€”often Venus, accompanied by Cupid. This tradition of representing abstract concepts as women is widespread:

- **Statue of Liberty**: A woman holding a torch, symbolizing freedom.
- **Justice**: A blindfolded woman with scales, representing fairness.
- **Venus**: A woman with Cupid, embodying love and beauty.

Art transforms intangible ideas into relatable human forms, using symbolic objects to reinforce meaning.

## Jan Bruegel the Elder: Artistic Lineage and Legacy

Before exploring the series of the five senses, it's essential to understand the legacy of Jan Bruegel the Elder and his collaboration with Rubens. Rubens painted a portrait of his friend Jan Bruegel with his family, highlighting Bruegel's reputation for capturing objects, textures, and flowers with remarkable detail. This artistic dynasty extended across generations: Jan's father, Pieter Bruegel the Elder; his brother, Pieter Bruegel the Younger; his son; and even his maternal grandparents, all contributed to the family's creative heritage.

One notable work is by Jan's maternal grandfather, Pieter Coecke van Aelst, who painted exquisite still lifes. His grandmother, Miken Verhus, specialized in miniatures and collaborated in her husband's workshop.

## Artistic Works and Themes: Transience in Still Life

Pieter Bruegel the Elder's "Massacre of the Innocents" is a powerful example of how themes can be altered by external forces. Emperor Rudolf II found the original subject too disturbing, prompting changes to the painting. The true theme survives in a copy by Pieter Bruegel the Younger.

![Massacre of the Innocents (Bruegel) - Wikipedia](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f6/Pieter_Bruegel_the_Elder_-_Massacre_of_the_Innocents_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg)
*Image: Massacre of the Innocents (Bruegel) - Wikipedia*

Jan Bruegel the Elder, meanwhile, became renowned for his still lifes, especially floral compositions. He meticulously depicted flowers from different seasons, fallen petals, and ephemeral insects, subtly reminding viewers of the fleeting nature of beauty and pleasureâ€”a central theme in Baroque art.

## Rubens and the Archducal Court: Patronage and Collaboration

Rubens painted two of his patronsâ€”Archduchess Isabella Clara Eugenia and Archduke Albertâ€”who were also Jan Bruegel's supporters. Isabella is depicted opening her fan, and Albert appears alongside her. Both feature in the series of the senses.

## The Senses: Renunciation and Allegory

Art also explores the senses through themes of renunciation. In Georges de La Tour's 1620 painting, Saint Jerome is depicted as a penitent, abandoning worldly pleasures and sensory stimulation. Penitent saints endure hunger, flagellation, and contemplation of death, viewing the senses as sources of pleasure to be forsaken for spiritual salvation.

## The Unicorn Tapestries: Medieval Allegory

The unicorn tapestries, woven around 1500, offer another perspective. Five tapestries represent the senses, while a sixth features a lady surrounded by animals and plants, with a unicorn. Here, sight is symbolized as the lady shows the unicorn its reflection in a mirror.

![Print The Lady and the Unicorn : Sight, ca. 1500 - 24x30cm ...](https://www.boutiquesdemusees.fr/files/products/31686/568128-73828bc2-xl/products-568128.jpg)
*Image: Print The Lady and the Unicorn : Sight, ca. 1500 - 24x30cm ...*

The unicorn, considered timid and only attracted by a virgin maiden, is lured and captured for its magical horn. Medieval allegories often used animal behavior to convey moral lessons, and the connection between the maiden and unicorn relates to the senses. Although the Latin words for the senses are masculine, the human figure representing each sense in Bruegel and Rubens' series is always a woman, echoing medieval associations with purity.

## Hearing and Sight in Baroque Art

Baroque artists often highlighted the senses through animals and objects. Cats and deer, known for their acute hearing, and birds with their melodious songs, are frequently depicted. Bells, horns, clocks, harps, and paintings fill the scene, reinforcing the sensory theme.

## The Sense of Hearing in Baroque Art

Baroque art often uses elaborate symbolism to evoke the senses, and hearing is no exception. In one striking diagram, a palace stands as the centerpiece, flanked by animals (cat, deer, birds), musical instruments (bells, horns, harps), and songbirds. Each element is carefully chosen to represent the auditory experience, weaving together nature and culture in a harmonious tableau.

### The Clavichord Scene: Music and Marvel

At the heart of this artistic exploration is a painted scene centered around a clavichord. Here, ordinary peopleâ€”astonished and attentiveâ€”gather with animals, all drawn to the music. These paintings, likely commissioned for the Archdukes Albert and Isabella Clara Eugenia, are modest in size (about a meter wide) but rich in detail. Remarkably, the musical score is rendered so precisely that it can be read directly from the canvas, underscoring the importance of music in the courtly life of the Archdukes.

In the Borghese Gallery, a micromosaic from 1618 by Marchelo Provenzale stands out. ## The Palace and Baroque Themes: From Myth to Everyday Life

Through the window, the palace belonging to the Archdukesâ€”painted by Jan Brueghel the Elderâ€”becomes a recurring motif. Unlike traditional mythological subjects such as Aphrodite or Mercury, these scenes feature ordinary people, sometimes using telescopes, subtly shifting the focus from legendary figures to the grandeur of architecture and scientific curiosity. The still life genre also emerges, with objects arranged without human presence, marking a significant innovation in Baroque art.

## The Annunciation Triptych: Hearing as Divine Reception

The Annunciation is a recurring theme, notably in the triptych by Robert Campin. Artists often use this scene to allegorize the sense of hearing, as Mary's conception is initiated by listening to the angel Gabriel. The spoken words are so powerful they extinguish a candle, agitating the air. White lilies, symbols of purity and virginity, also reference the color white and the trumpet shape, connecting visually and sonically to the theme of hearing.

## Medieval Reference: Annunciation to the Shepherds

The clavichord image is interpreted as the Annunciation to the shepherds, set against a golden medieval background. The angel proclaims, "He is born, go find him," and the Magi follow the star to the Nativity. The scene is rendered with a sense of wonder, capturing the Holy Family in the manger and the attentive shepherds with their sheep.

## The Sense of Sight: Cabinets of Curiosities and Collections

Transitioning to the sense of sight, Baroque art celebrates the cabinet of curiositiesâ€”a collection of rare and wondrous items amassed by those with economic power. When gallery space is exhausted, frescoes adorn the ceilings, as seen in the Farnese Gallery.

## Cabinets of Curiosities: The Origin of Museums

Small cabinets of curiosities, filled with disparate elementsâ€”sculptures, scenes, snails, jewelsâ€”mark the phenomenon of accumulation in the 15th century. By the 18th century, collections became specialized, with some collectors focusing on snails or animals. The distinction between "naturalia" and "artificialia" gave rise to natural science museums and art museums, with further divisions: European paintings to fine arts museums, African sculptures to ethnographic museums.
