## The Five Senses: Allegories and Still Life

Before delving into our series, let's consider an example: a work by Pieter Claesz, a Dutch artist from the Netherlands, who in 1623 painted the five senses. Sometimes, when contemplating works from this period, we may not realize they are allegories of the five senses, as today we simply know them as still lifes. In reality, artists are referencing the five senses, as seen here:

- **Hearing**: Music, represented by musical instruments.
- **Sight**: The painting itself, and the presence of a mirror reflecting a wine glass.
- **Touch**: The appearance of a turtle, referencing its hard, rough, and textured shell.
- **Taste**: Various foods, such as sliced bread, cake, sweets, and wine.
- **Smell**: Likely related to the same foods.

All five senses are depicted in the form of a still life.
![File:Claesz., Pieter - Still Life with Musical Instruments - 1623 ...](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/73/Claesz.%2C_Pieter_-_Still_Life_with_Musical_Instruments_-_1623.jpg)
*Image: File:Claesz., Pieter - Still Life with Musical Instruments - 1623 ...*

## Allegorical Representations

The painting we previously studied in class focused on hearing. As a concept, it is very different from the still life example. This is an allegory, where the allegorical figure is a woman, possibly Venus, accompanied by Cupid. There is always an allegorical representation of each sense through a female figure, generally Venus due to Cupid's presence.

Allegories represent specific concepts in human form. For example, the Statue of Liberty is depicted as a woman, and Justice as a blindfolded womanâ€”different forms of allegorical representation.

## Jan Bruegel the Elder and Artistic Dynasty

Before discussing the series of the five senses, let's talk about Jan Bruegel the Elder and Rubens, who collaborated on this series. Rubens painted a portrait of his friend Jan Bruegel with his family. Jan Bruegel, also known as Jan Velvet Bruegel, was famous for his depiction of objects, textures, and flowers, especially with his two children and wife, painted by Pieter Bruegel.
![The Family of Jan Brueghel the Elder - Courtauld](https://courtauld.ac.uk/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/P.1978.PG_.362.png)
*Image: The Family of Jan Brueghel the Elder - Courtauld*

Jan Bruegel the Elder belonged to a dynasty of paintersâ€”not only his father, Pieter Bruegel the Elder, but also his brother, Pieter Bruegel the Younger, his son, and even his maternal grandparents, who had artistic training. One notable work is by his maternal grandfather, Pieter Coecke van Aelst, who painted a beautiful still life. The grandmother, Miken Verhus, was also an artist, specializing in miniatures and collaborating in her husband's workshop.
![Triptych of the Adoration of the Magi, after Pieter Coecke van ...](https://www.anticstore.com/DocBD/commerce/antiquaire/costermans-antiquites-608/objet/124900/AnticStore-Large-Ref-124900.jpg)
*Image: Triptych of the Adoration of the Magi, after Pieter Coecke van ...*

When Jan Bruegel the Elder was born, his father died shortly after, and his mother passed away a few years later. Jan Bruegel and his brother Pieter Bruegel the Younger (known for infernal scenes) were raised by their grandmother, who provided their first artistic training. Later, Jan Bruegel trained in other workshops and traveled extensively in Italyâ€”Venice, Naples, Rome, Milanâ€”before returning to Antwerp.

The original version, preserved through a copy by Pieter Bruegel the Younger, shows the true subject matter.

## Rubens and the Archducal Court

Rubens depicted two of his patrons, who were also Jan Bruegel's patrons: Archduchess Isabella Clara Eugenia and Archduke Albert, rulers of the Spanish Netherlands. Isabella is shown opening her fan, painted by Rubens, and Albert is also depicted. They appear in the series of the senses.
![Isabella Clara Eugenia - Wikipedia](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/47/Isabella_Rubens.jpg)
*Image: Isabella Clara Eugenia - Wikipedia*

Jan Bruegel settled in Antwerp in 1596, and his children were Rubens' godchildren, highlighting their close relationship. From 1606, Jan Bruegel became a painter for the archducal court, and in 1613, he accompanied Rubens on a diplomatic mission to Holland. Rubens often interceded for him, and Jan Bruegel referred to Rubens as his "subsecretary" for resolving matters.

## Treatment of the Senses in Art

Before discussing the series, it is interesting to consider how the senses are treated. For example, in a 1620 painting by French artist Georges de La Tour, Saint Jerome is depicted as a penitent. Penitent saints abandon worldly noise and sensory stimulation, suffering and punishing the flesh to save the soul. They retreat, endure hunger, flagellate themselves, and contemplate death to be prepared for heaven. Here, the senses are seen as sources of pleasure to be renounced.

I researched ambergris to share with you. Ambergris is a waxy secretion produced by the digestive system of sperm whales. This substance floats in the sea, hardens over time, and develops a sweet aroma. It was used in perfumery as a fixative, similar to the civet's glands.
![Ambergris - Wikipedia](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2a/Ambergris%2C_Skagway_Museum.JPG)
*Image: Ambergris - Wikipedia*

Other substances burned in perfume diffusers include myrrh, a resin used for embalming. Myrrh, incense, and gold were expensive perfumed elements given as gifts by the Magi at Jesus's birth. Gold is a universal symbol of wealth, sandalwood is associated with rituals, and myrrh with embalming, as Jesus's destiny was death.
